Sentence Diagramming

Most methods of diagramming in pedagogy originated from the work of Alonzo Reed and Brainerd Kellogg in the late 1800â€™s. Instead of looking into the order of words, Reed-Kellogg diagrams took a different approach in that they focused more on how words function within a sentence and how they relate to each other. Sentence diagrams have stemmed from this approach and are now widely used by many to pictorially represent the grammatical structure of a sentence. They allow us to gain a better understanding of the relationship between words and the nature of syntactic structure. Diagramming sentences is also an effective method for creating more complex sentences. Sentence diagrams are used by teachers as a precise method of teaching grammar to students but can also be extremely beneficial to people trying to learn the English language.

Parts Of Speech

Every word in the English language belongs to one of eight word groups or parts of speech. Below is a brief summary of each group and how each can be represented within a sentence diagram.

Nouns

A noun denotes a person, place, animal, thing or idea. Nouns can perform a variety of jobs in sentences. They can act as subjects, direct and indirect objects, predicate nouns, and more!
Examples: John, Harvard, dog, computer, concept.

John walked the dog.
Harvard is located in Cambridge, Massachusetts.

Pronouns

A pronoun is a word that takes the place of one or more nouns. Pronouns can do everything that nouns can do and therefore can perform a variety of jobs in sentences also. There are three types of pronouns: object (for example, him); possessive (for example, his); or subject (for example, he).
Examples: I, me, you, he, she, they, we.

John saw his friend, and he waved at him.
She said that it is cold outside.

Adjectives

An adjective describes a noun or pronoun. They are words that describe or modify other words, making the English language a lot more interesting. Sentences can contain multiple adjectives.
Examples: the, special, blue, bright, colorful.

The blue car is John's.
She would like the fresh cake.

Verbs

A verb describes an action or a state of being. In English, a sentence or question cannot be made without a verb, this outlines the importance of these "action" parts of speech. Verbs always express activity.
Examples: cut, walk, run, swim, play, eat.

The baker cut the bread precisely. (action verb)
The bread is good. (linking verb)

Adverbs

An adverb is a word or phrase that describes a verb, adjective, or other adverbs. Adverbs perform a variety of functions in sentences.
Examples: very, rapidly, so, lazily, honestly.

The boy answered the question honestly.
She completed the task very quickly.

Prepositions

A preposition is a word that shows the relationship between a noun or a pronoun and some other word or element in the rest of the sentence. Prepositions occur in prepositional phrases: (a preposition + a noun or pronoun). These prepositional phrases act as adjectives or adverbs.
Examples: in, up, on, into, from, above, below.

John received a gift from Jill.
School finishes on Friday.

Conjunctions

A conjunction is a part of speech that is used to connect words, clauses, phrases, or sentences. There are multiple different types of conjunctions that perform various different tasks within sentence structures.
Coordinating conjunctions join two or more words, clauses, sentences, or other parts of speech with equal syntactic importance.
Examples: and, or, for, yet, but
Subordinating conjunctions join dependant clauses with independent clauses.
Examples: because, although, since.

Tom went to the zoo and to the cinema on Saturday.
The girl got good grades because she studied everyday.

Interjections

Interjections are words or phrases that show emotion. They are not grammatically related to the rest of sentence.
Examples: yeah, oh no, phew, ahh, eww

Oh no, he crashed the car!
Phew! That was close.

Diagram parts

There are a variety of primitives that can be combined to form the structural aspect of a sentence diagram. The examples on the display page demonstrate how these primitives can be used when diagramming a sentence. Below is a description of a selection of these primitives:

Base line: The base line can be used to extend an existing structure to make room for additional words or primitives. It can also be used when diagramming interjections separate from the main diagram of the sentence.

Subject-Verb relationship: This primitive is used to represent the subject and verb of a sentence. The subject is placed to the left of the middle divider and the verb to the right. In order to be complete, a sentence must contain a verb, and a verb must act on a subject so this primitive can be considered the core component of every diagram with all other components extending from it.

Direct object: A direct object takes the action of a verb in a sentence. This primitive is used to represent a direct object in a sentence diagram. As it takes the action of a verb, it extends from the verb in a diagram.

Indirect object: This primitive is used to represent an indirect object in a sentence diagram. An indirect object is the recipient of a direct object, meaning this primitive extends the verb in a sentence diagram.

Modifier: A modifier modifies another element in a sentence. Adjectives, adverbs and prepositional phrases are amongst the grammatical components that can act as modifiers. This primitive can be used within a sentence diagram to modify existing structures. They can act on the subject or nouns of a sentence and also act on the verbs.

Another modifier: This type of modifier can be used to extend existing modifiers. A simple example of where this primitive can be used is when two adverbs act on a verb. The first adverb extends from the verb in a sentence diagram and the second adverb extends from the first adverb. A sample sentence would be 'the boy ran very quickly' where 'very' and 'quickly' are the two adverbs that act on the verb.

Compound grouping: This primitive can be used to represent compound structures in a sentence diagram. As example of where it would be used is for a sentence that contains two subjects of equal importance.

Triple Grouping: Similar to a compound grouping, this primitive can be used to represent three structures of equal importance in a sentence diagram. A sample sentence that would require the use of this primitive would be 'David plays soccer, rugby and golf' where there are three objects of equal importance, 'soccer', 'rugby' and 'golf'.
